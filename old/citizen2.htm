Citizen Resources - Nuisance Abatement



Below is some background on the statute which provides information on how to identify a nuisance property.

This language could help identify properties in your neighborhood that would be candidates for the abatement process.

To begin the Nuisance Abatement process, contact City Attorney, Patrick Baker at patrick.baker@durhamnc.gov or 560-4158.

Civil Nuisance Abatement Actions

Introduction: North Carolina's nuisance abatement statutes, codified at G.S. Sections 19-1 et seq., were first enacted into law in 1913. However, for over eighty years, the statutes received sporadic use because abatement actions could only be brought by the Attorney General, district attorneys, or private citizens. An amendment to G.S. Section 19-2.1, which became effective on December 1, 1995, now permits cities and counties to initiate civil nuisance abatement actions in the name of the State of North Carolina. Thus, local governments are no longer dependent upon the initiative of either the Attorney General or district attorneys to bring abatement actions.

Identifying Nuisance Property

* The property focused upon may be either commercial or residential.

* Not all criminal activity can be used to support a civil nuisance abatement action. The State will need evidence of the following types of activity:

-Prostitution
-Gambling
-Illegal possession or sale of alcoholic beverages
-Illegal possession or sale of controlled substances
and/or
-Breaches of the peace such as homicide, assault, possession of dangerous or deadly weapons, discharging firearms, etc.

* Due to the language of the statutes, the types of activities listed above must have occurred repeatedly. While one time clearly is not enough, there is no "magic number." The number of occurrences needed to support a nuisance action will depend on the totality of the circumstances including, but not limited to, the nature of the calls (obviously a homicide would be given greater weight than a gambling offense) and the dispositions of the calls (a call for service which results in an arrest may be given greater weight than a call which is unfounded).

* The offenses referred to above must have occurred on the property or be directly connected to the property as in the case of a fight that started on the property and then spilled over onto the sidewalk or street.

Who May Bring An Action For Abatement

* Attorney General

* District attorney

* County or municipality upon approval of the County Commissioners or City Council

* Private citizen upon the posting of a $1000. bond

Nuisance Investigation, Procedure and Litigation

A civil nuisance abatement action is not a "quick fix." The investigation, preparation and disposition of a civil nuisance abatement action can be very time-consuming; involving months of work.

Assistance From the Public

* Call the police if you witness or suspect criminal activity. Oftentimes we find that people are reluctant to report certain crimes because they believe that the police will not be able to cite or arrest the suspect. For instance, a person may witness someone urinate on his or her lawn, but not call the police because the suspect will most likely be gone by the time the police are contacted and can arrive on the scene. While a citation or arrest may not result, contacting the police is very important because it creates a record for future evidentiary purposes.

* Notify your community liaison officer of property which you have identified as a potential nuisance under General Statutes Sections 19-1 et seq.

* Provide an affidavit for the State if requested to do so and be willing to testify at trial.

* If a nuisance action is pursued and a judgment is entered against the defendant, notify your community liaison officer of any activity that you believe may be a violation of the judgment. If the defendant violates a court order, he may be held in contempt of court.

Conclusion

If the State can establish a nuisance by a preponderance of the evidence, the court:

* must enter an order permanently enjoining the defendant and any other person from further maintaining the nuisance at the place complained of and elsewhere within the State

* must order that any lewd matter, illegal alcoholic beverages, gambling paraphernalia, and illegal controlled substances be destroyed and not sold;

* may require, with few exceptions, the effectual closing of the place against its use thereafter for the purpose of conducting any such nuisance

* may void the lease or other title held by a tenant or occupant

* must tax the costs of the action against the defendant.

However, if the defendant prevails, the costs of the action, including reasonable attorney´s fees, will be taxed against the State. In addition, a lost case may create unfavorable case law for future litigation.

Therefore, the initiation and pursuit of a civil nuisance abatement action should not be entered into lightly. It is usually an action reserved for situations in which traditional law enforcement methods and other administrative remedies (such as zoning, health and fire inspections) have failed.
